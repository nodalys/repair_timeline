Scenario 1

You are the National Coordination Forum of Country A (the regulators, the cable owners, and the operator).
Country A's submarine cable has been cut within its territorial sea by a ship anchor. It's causing internet slowdowns for businesses and millions of everyday users. The CS RepairShip can perform the repair. It is flagged to Country Z.
The CS RepairShip can be ready to sail within 24 hours, but the repair ship operator will not mobilise until they know they are able to get to the repair site, fearful of legal penalties or military interception.
a) Agency ping-pong — 3 days
The operator tries to work out which government ministry is responsible. They get passed around through 4 ministries.
b) Foreign ship in domestic waters — 7 days
Cable repair falls under cabotage laws. The ship is foreign-flagged. Country A's laws restrict foreign ships and crew from working in domestic waters.
c) Equipment import duties — 5 days
Country A treats spare cables and repeaters as standard commercial imports. Equipment held at port while duties are assessed.
d) Long processing times — 30 days
Applications for environmental, spatial, maritime, and security permits. Some are sequential, managed by different departments.

Scenario 1.5

⚠ SECOND FAULT — Network outage
Estimated impact: 200M per day
A fishing trawler has just cut through Country A's second submarine cable in Country A's EEZ. Both cables are now down. The internet is practically unusable.
Keep going with Question 1. Additionally...
e) Customs duties, taxes, and fees for EEZ transit — 3 days
Despite the fault being in the EEZ, Country A requires the vessel to make a detour and dock at port to clear immigration, pay port fees, and formally process the temporary importation of the subsea cable equipment spares. This adds 3 days in addition to the other processes in Question 1. Meanwhile the country continues to not have a usable internet.
